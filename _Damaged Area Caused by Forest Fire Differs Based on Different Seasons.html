How the Damaged Area Caused by Forest Fire Differs Based on Different Seasons¶

1 Introduction¶

1.1 Background¶

Past studies have shown that forest fires are an unavoidable natural phenomenon, and they're also increasingly dangerous as communities continue to spread away from urban areas. In 2018, nearly 60,000 fires burned parts of the continental United States. In November of that year alone, the fires killed more than 90 people and destroyed an estimated 14,000 homes and businesses (Linn, 2019). To prevent such detrimental consequences, it is necessary to understand the factors that are related to the degree of damage caused by forest fires.

One may ask if there is a relationship between the time of the year and the degree of damage inflicted by the forest fire. According to the data collected by the United States Environmental Protection Agency, from 1992 to 2015, the total acres burned by forest fires vary depending on different months of the year (Beckwith et al., 2018). This project aims to get an answer for this question: Do different months affect the area of damage caused by forest fires?

1.2 Dataset Description¶

We selected a forest fire data set from the Montesinho natural park, from the Tr´as-os-Montes northeast region of Portugal (P. Cortez & A. Morais, 2007). We will focus on the month of the year and the burned area (in ha). Among the months, we will use data for February and August as representative months for winter and summer, respectively, to compare the means of the damaged area between the two months. There are 20 and 183 observations for the February and August samples, respectively. We have decided to use data for February and August to represent winter and summer because they are the months that have the highest number of observations. We intend to choose the months with the greatest sample sizes to increase the precision of the results. There are only 9 and 2 observations for December and January, and these numbers are significantly lower than the sample size of the February group. Similarly, because there are only 17 and 32 observations data for June and July samples, we have decided to use the August data. The metric we are interested in is: damage area - month.

1.3 Questions¶

What do you expect to find?
Through this study, we can find if the forest fires yield different mean amounts of the damaged area in February and August.

What impact could such findings have?
This finding may help people with understanding the approximate extensions of damaged areas caused by forest fires in different months of the year and making appropriate responses to potential fire incidents.

What future questions could this lead to?
In the future, we may ask if the conclusion of the study would have been different if the data were collected in countries other than Portugal. Also, we may ask what specific factors are related to the degree of damage caused by forest fires.

Methods: Plan¶

The good thing about this report is that it suggests the method for conducting the hypothesis testing. We know that the February and August samples are from two independent populations but the standard deviation is unknown. Hence, we may conduct hypothesis testing with a two sample t-test.

According to the preliminary results, the mean damaged area caused by an individual forest fire case is observed to be higher in August compared to February by approximately 2.2 ha. However, to find out if the observed difference is statistically significant, we can conduct hypothesis testing at a 5% significance level, since this is a standard significance level.

The null hypothesis is that there is no difference between the mean damaged areas caused by the forest fires that occurred in February and August.

Parameters¶

µ1: True mean burned area caused be a forest fire incident occurred in August
µ2: True mean burned area caused be a forest fire incident occurred in February

Hypotheses¶

H0: µ1 - µ2 = 0
HA: µ1 - µ2 ≠ 0

Since the alternative hypothesis indicates that µ1 - µ2 is not equal to 0, the two-sided two sample t-test must be conducted. For the hypothesis testing, we will find the test statistic and the p-value using R in the Jupyter notebook to decide if the null hypothesis should be rejected or not.

2.2 Method¶

We chose a two-sample t-test in this situation since we are comparing the means of two independent populations (the burned area caused by forest fires in August and February). In addition, the population standard deviation is unknown. A two-sample t-test is useful for determining if the means of the two populations differ statistically significantly. In this example, the alternative hypothesis offers a two-sided test, which means we want to know if the difference between the means is significant, whether positive or negative. Therefore, a two-sided, two-sample t-test is the appropriate method for testing the hypothesis.

Since the sample size of the data is extremely small, only the bootstrapping method is appropriate for identifying certain numerical patterns and getting a convincing result. In this report, we conduct tests using the bootstrapping approach and calculate confidence intervals to determine the precision of our parameter value.

2.2.1 Test Statistic¶

In [8]:
# Assign names to the means, variances, and sample sizes
sample_mean_aug <- area_estimates$sample_mean[1]
sample_mean_feb <- area_estimates$sample_mean[2]
sample_var_aug <- area_estimates$sample_var[1] 
sample_var_feb <- area_estimates$sample_var[2]
sample_size_aug <- area_estimates$n[1] 
sample_size_feb <- area_estimates$n[2] 

# Calculate the test statistic
test_statistic <- 
  (sample_mean_aug - sample_mean_feb) / (sqrt(sample_var_aug/183 + sample_var_feb/20))

sprintf("The difference between the mean values of forest fire damage areas for the two months is: %.5f",test_statistic)
'The difference between the mean values of forest fire damage areas for the two months is: 0.65335'

2.2.2 P-value¶

In [9]:
# Find the p-value for hypothesis testing by applying the pt function
# The first term of the pt function is the test statistic. 
# The second term is the degree of freedom, which is the sum of two sample sizes minus one in this case.
# lower.tail must be set to False because this is a two-sided test.

p_value <- 2*pt(test_statistic, 183+20-1, lower.tail = FALSE)

sprintf("The p-value of difference in medians is: %.5f", p_value)
'The p-value of difference in medians is: 0.51428'

Since our p-value is higher than 0.05, we would not reject our null hypothesis.

The 95% bootstrap confidence interval that we found in section 2.2.4 is -4.89 and 8.29, and the range is roughly 13.19. The range of the confidence interval from the bootstrap method is only slightly smaller than that from the asymptotic method, and the two methods give confidence intervals with similar boundaries. Furthermore, our null hypothesis was that the difference between the two population means is equal to 0, and the confidence intervals from both methods contain 0. Since our p-value also indicates that our null hypothesis will not be rejected, the two confidence intervals are not significant. For these reasons, we cannot conclude if one method is more appropriate than the other, and we may choose either of these methods to interpret the confidence interval.

The 95% confidence interval from the asymptotic method is -4.41 and 8.82. This indicates that we are 95% confident that the difference in our population means falls between -4.41 and 8.82. In this case, the two population means indicate the means of the damaged area caused by individual forest fire incidents that occurred in February and August. As mentioned above, having 0 for the difference between the two population means was our null hypothesis, and our confidence interval further confirms that we would not reject our null hypothesis.

3 Discussion¶

Summarize what you found, and the implications/impact of your findings.

In this report, we tested the question of whether different months in different seasons impact the damaged area. We first looked at the descriptive measures of mean and standard deviation for these variables. We next performed hypothesis testing, using a bootstrap sample of 1000 replications of the 203 rows of data we obtained to run a two-sample two-sided t-test with a significance threshold of 5%. After conducting the t-test on the bootstrap sample, we got a p-value of 0.51, not rejecting our null hypothesis. We can conclude by saying that there is not enough evidence to support differences in damages done by forest fires during the winter and summer seasons of the year. To illustrate this, we have also plotted our results on a histogram with the null hypothesis model. Although the graph seems to be skewed, our results tend to lie less than two standard deviations away from the mean. At last, we evaluated two methods of calculating confidence intervals (bootstrapping and asymptotic). They have almost identical confidence intervals. Thus, the result obtained from the asymptotic method further proved that we cannot reject the null hypothesis.

As we found, we don't have sufficient evidence to suggest a significant difference between the average burned areas caused by forest fires in February versus those in August. There are various implications of this finding: for instance, This means that in terms of fire prevention and control, we don't need to pay too much attention to the month when the fire occurs. However, it should be noted that this does not mean that there is no difference in the degree of damage of the fire itself, but only illustrates the average damage area. Therefore, in practice, we still need to take appropriate prevention and control measures to minimize the threat of forest fires to the ecological environment and human society.

In addition to the average burnt area for individual forest fire events, there is no significant difference between winter and summer, we can also consider exploring the impact of other factors on fire damage, such as topography, forest vegetation type, precipitation before the fire and temperature, etc. These factors may have an impact on the scale, intensity, and burning speed of the fire, resulting in different sizes of burned areas. Therefore, when conducting further related research, we can consider comprehensive analysis from multiple aspects to more comprehensively and accurately assess the impact of different factors on forest fires.

If relevant, discuss whether your results were what you expected to find.

Unfortunately, our results were not something that we were expecting to find. We assumed that there would be more damage done by forest fires during summer seasons rather than winter. This is because we hear more about forest fires during summer seasons than in winter seasons. According to the Canadian Red Cross, most wildfires happen during the summer, burning nearly 2.5 million hectares of land. Although our data comes from Portugal, we assumed that the biggest factor to affect wildfires was seasons and not geography.

There could be several reasons why the results were not as expected. For instance, there could be other factors besides season and geography that influence the occurrence and severity of forest fires, such as human activity, temperature, humidity, precipitation, wind speed, topography, vegetation type, and fire suppression efforts. These factors may interact with each other in complex ways, making it difficult to isolate the effect of a single variable. Therefore, it is essential to consider these confounding variables and control for them in the study design and analysis.

Moreover, the data used in the study may not have been representative or comprehensive enough to capture the full range of variation in forest fires. For example, the data may have only included certain regions or types of forests, which may not generalize to other areas or ecosystems. Also, the data may have been collected using different methods or instruments at different times, introducing measurement errors or biases that could affect the results.

The other possible reason for the unexpected result may be the poor study design. Because of the significant difference between the sizes of the February and August samples, our variances for these two samples were different. For this reason, applying a t-test to evaluate our hypotheses might not have been appropriate. Instead of discarding our initial hypothesis, we may improve the study method and conduct more tests in the future.

Discuss future questions/research this study could lead to.

In the future, our study may undergo improvements in data collection. Our current research was conducted based on two samples with uneven numbers of observations; that is, the sample size of the August data is 183 while that of the February data is only 20. One of the conditions for using the t-model is having a normal distribution of data in both independent samples. If this condition is not satisfied, there should be sufficiently large sample sizes to validate the normal approximation. One may question if the sample size of 20 is large enough to apply a t-test to the skewed data. We have also noticed that the two groups have unequal variances, and this violates another condition for using a two-sample t-test. Because of the unequal variances, our t-test might not have been able to detect a statistically significant difference in mean damaged areas in August and February.

On top of that, Portugal does not experience as many wildfires as other countries such as Canada, the United States, Australia, or South Africa. There could also be other factors that can affect the amount of damage caused by wildfires such as the amount of vegetation within the region or whether there were systems in place by the local government in certain regions to lessen the spread of the wildfires. Thus, in the future, we may collect data from other regions of the world to make the samples more representative of the populations, and examine if the improved methods give us different conclusions to our study.

Future research can further explore the impact of climate change on the occurrence of forest fires, and assess the risk of wildfire disasters in different regions and environments. Secondly, wildfires have a wide range of impacts on the ecological environment and biodiversity. Therefore, future research can further explore the impact of wildfire on ecosystems, including the ability to recover and regenerate, and the impact on ecosystem function and structure. In a word, this study brings new ideas and research directions for the in-depth study of forest fires, and future research can be further expanded and deepened on this basis.
